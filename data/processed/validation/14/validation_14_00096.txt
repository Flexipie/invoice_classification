New methods of materials synthesis, such as combustion synthesis (1] and mechanical alloying (2] have
received considerable attention in recent years. High temperature combustion synthesis involves the
spontancous formation of a compound as a self-sustaining combustion wave rapidly propagates through the
powder reactants. In general, the reaction must be highly exothermic and a critical ignition temperature is
required to initiate combustion, In contrast, mechanical alloying is a low temperature powder process. It is
carried out in a high energy ball mill where repeated ball-powder-ball and ball-powder-container collisions
resuit inthe fracture and cold welding of the powder particles. Alloying takes place by inter-diffusion across
the surfaces joined by cold welding and is assisted by high defect densities resulting from the mechanical

Experimental Procedure

CuO and Ca powders were mechanically alloyed in a Spex 8000 mixer/mill using a hardened steel vial and
three 10mm diameter tungsten carbide balls. A 10% stoichiometric excess of calcium was used in all tests,
and the ball/powder mass ratio was 3:1. The vial was loaded and sealed in a dry nitrogen atmosphere. The
powders were milled for times up to 24h. The temperature during milling was measured using a type K
thermocouple inserted into a Imm diametes hole in the vial cover. The resulting powders were characterised
with a Rigaka X-ray diffractometer using CuKat radiation and examined in a Philips 505 SEM wi
EDAX ultra-thin window X-ray detector attatched. The ignition temperature for the reaction without
mechanical alloying was measured by embedding a thermocouple in a compacted cylinder of Ca and CuO
powders and heating the mixture in a vacuum at a tate of 20K/s until combustion occurred.
