SUBJECT: Use of Asparagine in Cigarette Products

We recently acquired information that Philip Morris has been purchasing
significant quantities of asparagine (possibly up to 30 metric tons per
year) for at least the last two or three years. Our conjecture is that
it is being used for reaction flavor development. A literature search
identified a Philip Morris patent, U.S. 4,379,464, 1983, describing the
reaction of reducing sugars with ammonium hydroxide or ammonium salts
in the presence of trace amounts of amino acids, including asparagine,
to produce reaction flavors for use in smoking products. A separate
reference, Koehler and Odell, J. Agr. Food Chem. 18, No. 5, 895,
(1970), describes the production of alkylpyrazine compounds from sugar~
amine acid reactions, Greatest yields were attained utilizing
asparagine as the amino acid.

The sugar-amine acid reaction occurs at temperatures above 100 degrees
Celcius. This indicates three immediately apparent possibilities: (1)
The reaction flavors are produced in vitro and applied to tobacco
material; (2) The reaction constituents are placed on tobacco material
and dryers utilized to drive the reaction; and/or (3) The reaction
constituents are applied to tobacco material and the heat of combustion
during smoking utilized to drive the reaction.

Competitive Brands reports that RJR's exoding competitive position
versus Philip Morris is expected to worsen over the next three years
according to the most recent Operating Plan Update. RJR's inability to
penetrate the young adult smoker market and its disadvantage in the
lights/low tar category are keys to this decline. Neither critical
area can be totally explained by RIR advertising or marketing
strategies, nor can these areas be explained by routine analytical
testing which has not been helpful in identifying meaningful
differences between RJR and PM products. Therefore, product
differences which current methods cannot identify must account for some
of this dilemma.

Based on the above information, APT, Competitive Brands, and S. Simmons
propose preliminary research which may identify flavor differences that
account for some of our competitive disadvantages and form a basis for
development work for improved performance in the described critical
areas. We plan to begin the investigation through the following
