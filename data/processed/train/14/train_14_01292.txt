LICORICE

Background. Licorice or glycyrrhiza is derived from
the rhizomes and roots of a perennial leguminous wild plant,
Glycyrrhiza glabra L., native to Southern Europe and Central
Asia. The roots are best suited for harvesting after 4 to 6
years of growth and contain from 6 to 14 percent glycyrrhizic
acid or glycyrrhizin. Spanish and Italian roots tend to con~
tain lower amounts of glycyrrhizin than Oriental roots (Houseman,
1922-23; Muller, 1965}. The licorice root is the only known
vegetation to contain significant quantities of glycyrrhizin.

Besides glycyrrhizin, licorice contains reducing
sugars and nonreducing sugars, starch, plant gums, resins,
flavonoids, essential oils, inorganic salts and low levels of
nitrogenous constituents such as proteins, amino acids, and
nucleic acids (Muller, 1965; Vora, 1984; Frattini, 1977). The
licorice root yields licorice in a variety of products, includ-
ing block licorice, licorice powder, spray dried powder, drum
aried pulverized pellets, liquid extracts and glycyrrhizin
formulations.
