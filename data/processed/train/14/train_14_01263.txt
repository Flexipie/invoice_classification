‘SUMMARY

This study was conducted to investigate the possible causes of padding observed ("3 in CPCL
bales. The effects of moisture, packing temperature and packing density of CPCL was studied,
along with the related effect of storage conditions. The conditions selected were within the
‘operating limits (moistures, temperatures, storage conditions and packing density) observed

istorically at Lancaster. The resuits indicate that high moistures and high packing density
significantly increases the incidence of padding, At conditons of high moisture and high
packing density, about 13% by weight of the bales was observed as pads as compared to 6%
padding under nomal moisture and packing density (Table 1). The interaction of moisture and
packing density also contribute significantly to padding. However, the stfects of higher packing
temperatures and storage conditions (refrigerated vs ambient) were not found to be significant
(or the conditions studied. To substantially reduce the incidence of CPCt padding from the
current 8% level would require either low packing moistures (11%) of elimination of prizing. It
should be noted that with normal packing density, high packing moisture does not significantly
increase the incidence of padding as compared to packing at normal moisture.
