Abstract

‘The distribution of nicotine among its states of ionization in tobacco smoke has
been commonly treated as an equilibrium calculation requiring only knowledge of pH
and the ionic equilibrium constants involved. Such an approach does not consider the
consequences that follow if smoke is recognized to bea system that evolves in time as
volatile components pass from the aerosol particles to the gas phase. A mathematical
model of an aqueous solution of ammonia, acetate, and nicotine has been developed
to simulate the volatile loss of these components from tobacco smoke. In the simula-
tions the high volatility of ammonia relative to that of the other components causes its
tapid toss from the system. This shortens the time interval within whic ammonia can
affect the pH of the system, leading to rates of loss of acetate and nicotine which are
quite different from those expected on the basis of equilibrium calculations alone.
